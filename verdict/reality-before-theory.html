The Verdict // Principle 03

REALITY
BEFORE THEORY

I watched eighteen stakeholders across eight functional domains walk into a three-day internal summit, every one of them correct about their own constraints and almost none of them seeing anyone else's.

Operations knew the new distribution center wasn't ready yet. Finance had no SKU-level margin visibility. Marketing knew its attribution data was incomplete. The web dev team didn't have a clear scope of the planned ERP migration. All true simultaneously.

The directive: a unified roadmap. But, where do we want to be in three years? That's a fine questions if you already have a shared understanding of what's actually possible. They didn't. Asking people to dream before they've agreed on reality produces roadmaps that collapse the moment someone names a constraint the room wasn't ready to hear.

So I started upstream. Not with vision. With limits. What can't you do right now? What breaks if we try? Who owns this and does everyone (or anyone) agree? I needed every team to stop presenting preferred version of reality and start disclosing what they were actually living with.

The summit worked because the first day was dedicated entirely to pressure testing. Not solving. Not planning. Just surfacing where things would break. It was explicitly acceptable to let initiatives die if the stakeholders couldn't defend them against the real limitations in the room.

I grew up in small-town, rural Missouri. A town where honesty mattered more than polish and people worked with what they had. The newsroom reinforced it daily and for years. You report what happened, not what you or anyone else wanted to happen. You go to the scene. You talk to the people who were there. You don't write the story before you've done the reporting.

The corporate world has a strange tolerance for skipping steps. Roadmaps built on what leaders wants to be true rather than what the teams on the ground can actually execute. The map gets drawn first, then everyone is surprised when the terrain doesn't match.

All of the durable things I've built started with assessing the extreme, upsteam reality first.
